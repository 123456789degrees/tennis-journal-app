## Open-ended phase guide

Question areas for the problem-discovery half. Broad → specific. **Bullet points, not a script — don't read these aloud.**

> Reminders:
> - Ask about past behavior, not hypotheticals. "When was the last time…" beats "would you ever…".
> - Don't ask feature questions ("would you want a button that…") or validation questions ("isn't this annoying?"). They produce nodding, not learning.
> - Don't pitch MatchBook in this phase. Just listen.
> - Let silence be okay — give them time to think.

- **Playing someone again (tests: repeat opponents, recall)**
  - "When's the last time you played someone you'd played before? Tell me about it."
  - "How often does that happen in a season — facing the same people?"
  - "Did you remember how they played from last time, or did you have to figure it out again?"

- **The match itself / tactical gap (tests: the need)**
  - "Tell me about a recent match you felt you should've won but didn't. What happened?"
  - "Has there been a time you knew an opponent's weakness but couldn't figure out how to use it?"
  - "Walk me through what was going through your head when it wasn't working."

- **Preparing for a match (tests: competitive alternative)**
  - "Last time you had a match coming up against someone specific, what did you do to get ready?"
  - "Do you ever talk to anyone about how an opponent plays? Who, and when was the last time?"

- **Recording / remembering (tests: journaling friction, need)**
  - "After a match, do you ever write anything down? Tell me about the last time you did — or didn't."
  - "If you don't, what happens to all that info about the match?"

- **Existing tools (tests: competitive alternative, unhappy with workaround)**
  - "Have you ever used an app or notebook to track matches or opponents? What happened with it?"
  - "What did you like or not like about it?"

## Solution feedback phase guide

Only enter this AFTER the player has described the problem in their own words. If they never showed the problem, skip this — that interview was about the problem, not the solution.

- **What to show, in what order:**
  - First the opponent lookup: "imagine you type in an opponent's name and it shows their playstyle and a plan for how to beat them." See if they get it without explanation.
  - Then the automatic practice page: "and separately, it looks at your recent matches on its own and tells you what to work on in practice."
- **What to listen for:**
  - Confusion or "wait, how would it know that?" — more useful than "that's cool."
  - Whether they immediately picture a specific opponent / situation they'd use it for.
  - **The #1 risk:** do they believe the AI tips would actually be good and worth trusting — or do they say their coach already tells them this, or that they wouldn't trust an app's advice?
- **Prompts:**
  - "Show me how you'd actually use this — what would you do first?"
  - "What would make you trust (or not trust) what it tells you?"
  - "When in your week would you actually open this?"

## Closing

- Thank them for their time.
- "Is there anyone else you think I should talk to about this?"
- "Okay if I come back with a follow-up question if something comes up?"

## Falsification commitments

**Written before the interviews. The "what actually happened" lines get filled in afterward.**

### Interview 1 — Wylan (planned: this week, in person)

#### Target customer (repeat opponents)

- **Current belief:** Junior USTA players face the same opponents repeatedly over a season.
- **Confirms it if:** Wylan names specific people he's played multiple times and treats it as normal.
- **Refutes it if:** He says he almost never plays the same person twice.
- **Complicates it if:** It happens, but rarely — only at the bigger tournaments or only a couple of rivals.
- **What actually happened:** CONFIRMED. Wylan plays the same opponents multiple times. Target-customer assumption (repeat opponents within a section) holds for him.

#### Need or opportunity (recall + tactical gap)

- **Current belief:** Players forget opponents over long gaps and/or know a weakness but not how to exploit it.
- **Confirms it if:** He describes forgetting an opponent's game over time, OR knowing a weakness but not the tactic to use it.
- **Refutes it if:** He says he remembers opponents fine and always knows what to do tactically.
- **Complicates it if:** One half is true but not the other (e.g., remembers fine but lacks tactics, or vice versa).
- **What actually happened:** LEANS REFUTED / COMPLICATED for an experienced player. Wylan said he "mostly knows how to beat that already" — the tactical-gap need is weak for him. He is tactically experienced, so the core pain Joshua feels does not transfer to him.

#### Key benefit / differentiation (the AI tips)

- **Current belief:** AI tips on how to beat an opponent + what to practice are genuinely valuable to players like him.
- **Confirms it if:** When shown the concept, he reacts with real interest and points to a situation he'd use it in.
- **Refutes it if:** He shrugs, says his coach already covers this, or says he wouldn't trust app-generated tips.
- **Complicates it if:** He's into the opponent-prep but not the practice tips (or the reverse).
- **What actually happened:** COMPLICATED. He said the AI-tips idea is "fine" but lukewarm — he mostly already knows how to beat opponents. Signal: the differentiation may matter much more for players with LESS tactical knowledge than for experienced players. (Joshua's read: app is for less-experienced players. NOTE: this is a NEW hypothesis to test, not a confirmation — an on-paper target customer partially refuted the value.)

#### Journaling friction (part of the need)

- **Current belief:** Players don't keep match notes because doing it manually is a chore.
- **Confirms it if:** He also doesn't journal / finds it annoying or pointless.
- **Refutes it if:** He already keeps notes happily and finds them useful.
- **Complicates it if:** He used to but stopped, or does it inconsistently.
- **What actually happened:** CONFIRMED (after clarification). Wylan keeps a physical journal, but ONLY because his mom forces him — he doesn't actually like it. This mirrors Joshua's origin story (parent-pushed, disliked). The manual-journaling pain is real and parent-driven, not chosen.
