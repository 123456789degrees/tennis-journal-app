## What this step was

Research and personal experience can only get you so far — the next step was testing the idea's assumptions against real people in the target audience: junior competitive tennis players. The goal of an interview like this isn't to get compliments on the idea, it's to find out which parts of it are actually wrong. Each interview went in with a specific "falsification commitment" — a stated way the idea could be proven false.

Two players were interviewed: **Wylan** and **Daren**, both junior competitive players (interviewed together, so treated as a correlated rather than fully independent sample — a limitation carried forward honestly rather than hidden).

## Result

<div class="results-box">
<h3>What got confirmed</h3>
<ul>
<li>Both players face the same opponents repeatedly across a season — the core assumption the whole "remember your opponents" feature depends on.</li>
<li>Both keep a physical journal <em>only</em> because a parent makes them, and both dislike it — the exact journaling pain the product is built around.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="results-box">
<h3>What got refuted (and changed the product)</h3>
<ul>
<li>The original idea assumed AI tactical tips ("how to beat this opponent") would be the main draw. Both interviewees were lukewarm on this — experienced players mostly already know their opponents' games.</li>
<li>This directly caused the positioning pivot described on the <a href="{{ '/positioning-statement.html' | relative_url }}">Positioning Statement</a> page: the headline benefit moved from tactical AI advice to effortless capture + recall, with AI tips kept as a bonus rather than the main pitch.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="callout">
<h3>Still open</h3>
<p>Because both interviews were with strong, experienced players, it's still untested whether AI tactical tips matter more to a <em>less</em> experienced player — someone who doesn't already know how to read an opponent. That's flagged as an open question rather than resolved.</p>
</div>
